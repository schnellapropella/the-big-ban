If social media amplifies problems,
will banning it solve them?

What large-scale data reveals about social media, personal risk factors, and the limits of platform bans for children under 16.

By Honor Butler, Jacklyn O'Brien & Gabriele Blaschko · April 2026

✦ Personal

I was born in 2004. I've never known a world without the internet.

Childhood and teen memories with social media are positive. Instagram and Snapchat were the main platforms used, still popular with my friends today. If you told me that social media was to be banned when I was a teen, it would have caused upset.

I was born in 2004 in Surrey, UK, part of the generation called the “Digital Natives”. The internet was already around for more than 20 years. I’ve never known a world without digital devices and online spaces, learning to navigate them from very young.

◈ Background

Australia banned social media for under-16s. The world is watching.

Last December, Australia implemented a worlds-first law banning social media for under-16s. It sparked global debate, and many countries are considering following suit.

The start of a new era of trouble-free childhoods?

It's not that simple. Difficulties start with varying definitions of what social media even means across the world. In addition, many countries already have regulations in place, such as the UK's Online Safety Act. However, public pressure is mounting because this law is considered insufficient for protecting children, fuelling calls for an Australian style ban in the UK.

The proposed bill is locked in parliamentary limbo. The House of Commons voted against a ban in March 2026, while the House of Lords rejected the MP’s alternatives twice. The bill returns to the Commons on 15 April.

◈ Academic Research

What is it actually based on?

It often seems the science on social media’s effect on children’s wellbeing is settled. It isn't. Decades of research show highly mixed results, varying by what is measured, how, and in whom. The EU’s Joint Research Centre warns of “inconsistent findings,” unreliable self-reports and a near-total absence of causal evidence.

"What we currently lack is strong scientific evidence on whether banning social media for certain ages is an effective way [in reducing harm on children]."

— Professor Amy Orben, University of Cambridge

Part of the problem is definition: what counts as “use”? Does scrolling equal use, or only active engagement? As Katrine Tølbøll, PhD candidate at Aarhus University , puts it: "Are you watching cute cat videos or stalking your ex-boyfriend? These are different experiences but collapse into the same thing, which makes it very hard to research."

Ultimately, research suggests wellbeing is shaped less by platforms than by what children bring to them: their health, relationships and free time.

◉ The Data

The variables that change everything

To understand the impact personal-level factors have on a children’s experience on social media, we have taken data from the longitudinal survey “Understanding Society” . It captures children’s own evaluations of their lives, ages 10-15, with a total of 15 waves, years 2010-2024. We have taken out two panels , 2010-2014 and 2015-2019. Each panel tracks the same children over a 5-year period. Tracking the same children over time controls for unobserved factors of the participants, improving the credibility of results.

Panel 2 shows both higher social media usage and a sharper decline in happiness. This aligns with the rapid expansion of social media platforms during the 2010s. We can therefore infer that, among today’s youth, both social media use and declining happiness are continuing to rise.

◉ The Data

What would the data say about Honor?

Active leisure activities, strong family support, good health — these are the variables that characterise Honor's profile in the dataset. And they are also, consistently, the variables most associated with resilience to any negative effects of social media.

◈ Discussion

Same platform, different lives, different outcomes.

What does the data mean? Personal traits and life circumstances shape how young people experience social media; the platform itself is not decisive. It reflects and amplifies existing vulnerabilities rather than creating them, intensifying what is already there. The same scroll that leaves one teenager feeling connected can leave another feeling isolated.

Not only is not all use equal, but children are also not equally at risk of being heavily affected. Problematic use, compulsive behaviour disrupting sleep, school and relationships, is linked not just to screen time, but to pre-existing factors like poor mental health, neurodivergence and adverse experiences.

The child most at risk is not the average user. They are a child already struggling before they open an app.

"Bans don't really work on problematic social media use. They're so addicted they will find a way around it — a VPN, a parent's phone. It will be the ordinary kid affected, the one who is doing pretty decent."

— Katrine Tølbøll, Aarhus University

This concern is noted in the scientific community. A ban, as Sonia Livingstone argues, leaves the exploitative algorithmic and data practices of big platforms untouched. UNICEF warns that age-based restrictions are unlikely on their own to drive the changes the technology industry needs to make, and risk failing to account for the diversity among children and varying circumstances.

Pushing under-16s off mainstream platforms does not make them safer — it may redirect them toward less regulated spaces with fewer protections. Education minister, Olivia Bailey told the Guardian “Others, including children’s charities, have warned that a blanket ban could drive children towards less regulated corners of the internet or leave teenagers unprepared when they do come online.” It shifts responsibility away from the companies that profit from these platforms, as Dr Margarita Panayiotou from the University of Manchester warns.

The question is not whether children need better protection online. They do. The question is if an age-based ban is the correct response to a problem the evidence tells us is far more nuanced than that.

◉ What now?

If a ban is not the answer, what is?

The evidence points toward a different set of priorities. Algorithms need to be regulated, not just the age children can access them. As Katrine Tølbøll argues: "Some of the algorithms are way too powerful [...] many of them are created to become addictive and make people engage in problematic use with social media." The 5Rights Foundation told the Guardian social media companies must not be “let off the hook” by a ban which many children are likely to dodge.

The UK already has good legislation in the Online Safety Act, but it has not been implemented correctly. The NSPCC has warned that a blanket ban risks "driving teenagers into darker, unregulated corners of the internet." UNICEF calls for a smart mix of measures: platform-level regulation, algorithm transparency, and crucially, children themselves being included in decisions that affect them.

Implementing bans without consulting children may itself violate their rights under Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

Social media is not the cause.
What it reveals, a ban cannot fix.
