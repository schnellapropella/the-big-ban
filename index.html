If social media amplifies problems,
will banning it solve them?

What large-scale data reveals about social media, personal risk factors, and the limits of platform bans for children under 16.

By Honor, Ella & Fiona · April 2026

✦ Personal

I was born in 2004. I've never known a world without the internet.

Born in the early 2000s, I am part of the generation others call the "Digital Natives." In 2004, the internet had already been around for more than 20 years. It was the year Facebook was founded. Myspace was the coolest social media site and the first to reach one million users.

I learned to navigate digital spaces from a young age. Looking back on my childhood and adolescence, my memories with social media are predominantly positive. Instagram and Snapchat were the main platforms, and are still popular among my friends and me today. If you told me social media was to be banned when I was a teenager, I know it would have caused real upset.

◈ Background

Australia banned social media for under-16s. The world is watching.

Access to social media is something our generation takes for granted. For the next generation, that could soon change. Last December, Australia became the first country in the world to ban social media for under-16s — no Instagram, no Snapchat, no YouTube. It sparked a global debate, and many countries are now considering following suit.

But it's not that simple. Even the definition of "social media" is contested: which platforms count? What about AI or gaming? Across the world, every country is doing its own thing — Australia itself has had to revise its list of blocked platforms several times. Many countries already have regulations in place, such as the EU's Digital Services Act and the UK's Online Safety Act, but public pressure is mounting because existing laws are seen as insufficient to protect children.

In the UK, researcher Sonia Livingstone of the LSE argues that the failure to implement the Online Safety Act effectively is precisely what has fuelled calls for a ban. Meanwhile, the UK's own 'Children's Wellbeing and Schools Bill' is locked in parliamentary limbo — MPs voted against a ban in March 2026, while the House of Lords has twice rejected the government's alternatives. The bill returns to the Commons on 15 April.

"We do not yet know if those children are happier or lonelier, more or less supported, nor whether they have found their way to even riskier platforms."

— Sonia Livingstone, LSE

UNICEF warns of a "significant risk that age-based restrictions alone will fail to address the underlying drivers of harm," and calls instead for a "smart mix" of measures — platform regulation, algorithm transparency, and meaningful input from children themselves.

◈ Academic Research

The science is not settled. And that inconclusiveness is itself the finding.

Let's look at what the data actually shows. Using the University of Essex's "Understanding Society" survey, we identified a link between higher social media use and a decline in happiness. While the effect was statistically significant, its effect size is negligible in real-life terms — meaning other variables needed to be interrogated.

This reflects the broader state of the field. If you follow the public debate, you might assume the science is settled. It isn't. Decades of research have produced highly heterogeneous results, with effects varying in direction, size and significance depending on what is measured, how, and in whom. More rigorous longitudinal studies consistently find smaller effects than the cross-sectional designs that dominate the literature.

The EU's Joint Research Centre warns explicitly of "inconsistent findings," unreliable self-reported data, and a near-total absence of causal evidence. The assumption that more time on social media leads proportionally to worse outcomes — the so-called dose-response model — has been identified as scientifically inadequate.

"Are you watching cute cat videos or stalking your ex-boyfriend? These are different experiences but collapse into the same thing — which makes it very hard to research."

— Katrine Bønneland Tølbøll, Aarhus University

What the research does suggest is that the relationship between social media and wellbeing is shaped less by the platform itself than by what a child brings to it: their health, their relationships, their circumstances. The digital space acts less as a cause — and more as an amplifier.

✦ Personal

I remember setting up my first Instagram account with my dad.

I wanted to post pictures of random things I liked and share them with friends. My parents were on board — the app was relatively new and seemed pretty innocent. No DMs, no influencers advertising products or encouraging dangerous behaviours. I made a secondary account where I posted pictures of nail designs. Looking back, it could not have been more innocent.

I was also a very busy child and teenager. I did lots of sports — swimming, netball — and spent six hours at theatre school every Saturday.

"We could afford for you to have a lot of extra curricular activities and I totally appreciate that lots of families can't afford that. You feel differently if your child plays computer games on a Saturday afternoon if they have done three hours of netball in the morning."

— Fiona, Honor's mum

Everyone's experience with social media is different. Although mine was positive, looking at the panel data in which I fit, it is clear how different variables have caused very different outcomes for other teenagers in the same years. Now let's look at what the data says.

◉ The Data

Happiness and social media use over time

We used data from the "Understanding Society" longitudinal survey by the University of Essex and the Institute for Social and Economic Research. It captures children's own evaluations of their lives, ages 10–15, across 15 waves from 2010–2024.

We focused on two panels — 2010–2014 and 2015–2019 — each tracking the same children over four years. Tracking the same children over time controls for unobserved individual factors, improving the credibility of results.

◈ Discussion

The variable is not the platform. It is what the child brings to it.

Personal traits and life circumstances — health, sex, family background — shape how a young person experiences social media. The platform itself is not the decisive variable. What a child brings to it is.

This is the amplification argument. Social media does not create vulnerabilities from scratch. It reflects and intensifies what is already there. The same scroll through Instagram that leaves one teenager feeling connected and seen can leave another feeling isolated and inadequate. Same platform, different lives, different outcomes.

What researchers call problematic social media use — compulsive, uncontrolled engagement that interferes with sleep, schoolwork and relationships — is predicted not by time spent online, but by what a child brings to the screen: pre-existing poor mental health, low self-esteem, ADHD symptoms, adverse childhood experiences, weak social connections. The child most at risk is not the average user. They are a child already struggling before they ever open an app.

"Bans don't really work on problematic social media use. They're so addicted they will find a way around it — a VPN, a parent's phone. It will be the ordinary kid affected, the one who is doing pretty decent."

— Katrine Bønneland Tølbøll, Aarhus University

A ban, as LSE's Sonia Livingstone has argued, leaves the exploitative algorithmic and data practices of the big platforms entirely untouched. Prof Amy Orben of Cambridge is direct: "What we currently lack is strong scientific evidence on whether banning social media for certain ages is an effective way of achieving this." UNICEF warns that age-based restrictions are unlikely on their own to drive the changes the technology industry needs to make.

Pushing under-16s off mainstream platforms does not make them safer — it may redirect them toward less regulated spaces with fewer protections. It shifts responsibility away from the companies that profit from these platforms, and risks distracting from the interventions the evidence actually supports.

The question is not whether children need better protection online. They do. The question is whether a blunt, age-based ban is a proportionate response to a problem the evidence tells us is far more nuanced than that.

◉ What now?

If a ban is not the answer, what is?

The evidence points toward a different set of priorities. Algorithms need to be regulated — not just the age at which children can access them. As Katrine Bønneland Tølbøll argues: "Some of the algorithms are way too powerful… many of them are created to become addictive and make people engage in problematic use with social media."

The UK already has good legislation in the Online Safety Act — but it has not been implemented correctly. The NSPCC has warned that a blanket ban risks "driving teenagers into darker, unregulated corners of the internet." UNICEF calls for a smart mix of measures: platform-level regulation, algorithm transparency, and — crucially — children themselves being included in decisions that affect them.

Implementing bans without consulting children may itself violate their rights under Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

"Social media is not the cause. It is a mirror —
and what it reflects, a ban cannot fix."
